Data Analysis

A deep dive into some factors which affect success in the NFL

Most Expensive Player

The first question we asked was whether the most expensive player on a roster affects how often a team wins. It would seem as though truly elite players command higher salaries. So paying up for elite players may lead to more wins. On the other hand, the more you pay one player - usually the Quarterback - the less you have to spread amongst the rest of the roster. So maybe paying LESS for your highest player yeilds more wins.

Lets take a look:

In the regular season, it appears as though spending more on your best player does, in fact, yeild more wins. There seems to be a connection between wins and the amount a team spends on its best player.

In the playoffs, the chart looks very different. Here, it looks as though wins increase with the salary of your highest paid player - to a point. But after your highest paid player earns more than 13% of the salary cap, a team experiences diminishing returns.

In the 12 seasons worth of data we have, we see that, when a player earns more than 13% of the salary cap, his team won the superbowl only once (2022 KC Chiefs - Pat Mahomes). If the 49 teams who've paid their highest player more than 13%, we see only 22 wins and 1 superbowl.

Depth of Talent

Ok, so if paying your best player has an upper limit of success at 13% of the cap, What about teams that spread their money more equitably? Do teams that give MORE players a higher percentage of the salary cap prove more successful?

Lets take a look:

It apears as though having MANY mid-level salaries on a roster leads to more success than having one extravigant salary. As teams find more players worth 2% of the cap or more, their expected wins climb from 5 to 12. Bulding a team of good-to-great players is a viable means for success.

Spending by Offense/Defense

What about for each side of the ball? Does spending on offense translate to more wins? Or does the old addage "Defense Wins Championships" hold true?

Lets take a look:

Spending money on offense OR defense contributes to wins. You can see that there is a positive correlation between money spent on offense and defense to wins. However, the line for offensive spending is steeper, indicating that you get more "bang" for your buck by spending on the offensive side of the ball.

What About In Playoffs?

Does spending money on offense translate to wins in the playoffs?

Lets take a look:

One of the most surprising findings of this study is the inefficicency in defense spending come playoff time. At best there is no correlation between defensive spending and playoff wins. And it's possible that spending more money on defense may hurt a teams chances of winning addiional playoff games. Teams that invest only 20-25% of their budgets fair just as well in the playoffs as teams spending much more!
